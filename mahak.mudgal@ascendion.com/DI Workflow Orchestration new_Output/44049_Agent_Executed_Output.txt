SECTION 1 — WITH AVAILABLE AGENTS

Option 1: Conceptual Data Model Development Workflow

1. Agents to consider:
   Fabric Model Conceptual → Fabric Model Data Constraints

2. What we will get:
   • A complete conceptual data model for credit card reporting that includes all core business entities (Application, Campaign, Credit Risk, Fraud Screening, Transaction, Funnel Stage) with their attributes, relationships, and primary keys, derived directly from the credit card reporting requirements document. The Fabric Model Conceptual agent will analyze the requirements document to extract and define these entities, establish their relationships (such as Application-to-Customer, Transaction-to-Account, Campaign-to-Application), and produce a conceptual entity-relationship diagram that supports all five reporting objectives (application funnel analysis, campaign performance tracking, credit risk assessment, fraud detection, and transaction monitoring).

   • A comprehensive set of data constraints, business rules, and validation logic for each entity and attribute defined in the conceptual model. The Fabric Model Data Constraints agent will process the conceptual model output to define mandatory/optional attribute rules, data type constraints, referential integrity rules, calculation formulas (such as approval rates, conversion metrics, risk scores), data quality expectations, and compliance-related constraints (PII handling, data retention policies, audit trail requirements). This ensures that the conceptual model is not only structurally complete but also operationally enforceable with clear business logic.

   • Full traceability and documentation linking each entity, attribute, relationship, and business rule back to the original requirements document, enabling stakeholders to validate that all reporting needs are addressed. Both agents will produce detailed rationale documents explaining modeling decisions, assumptions made, ambiguities identified, and gaps escalated, ensuring transparency and auditability throughout the conceptual modeling process. This documentation will support downstream logical and physical modeling phases and facilitate governance reviews.

3. What we will miss:
   • Explicit domain-bounded context modeling and subject-area cataloguing that would provide a higher-level organizational structure for the conceptual model. While the Fabric Model Conceptual agent identifies entities and relationships, it does not explicitly define domain boundaries (such as Customer Domain, Product Domain, Transaction Domain) or create a formal subject-area hierarchy that would help enterprise architects understand how the credit card reporting model fits within the broader enterprise data architecture. This gap means that cross-domain dependencies, shared entities, and enterprise-wide data governance alignment may require additional manual analysis.

   • A formal business glossary with standardized definitions, synonyms, and business ownership metadata for each entity and attribute. Although the Fabric Model Conceptual agent will list entities and attributes with descriptions, it does not produce a structured glossary artifact that includes business-approved definitions, alternative names used across departments, data stewardship assignments, and lineage to enterprise-wide master data. This means that business users and downstream teams may face ambiguity in terminology, and the model may not align with existing enterprise glossary standards without additional manual curation.

   • Explicit validation of business goals, KPIs, and use-case prioritization against stakeholder expectations before modeling begins. While the Fabric Model Conceptual agent analyzes reporting requirements to derive entities, it does not include a formal step to confirm with business stakeholders that the identified goals (such as improving approval rates, reducing fraud losses, optimizing campaign ROI) and their relative priorities are correctly understood and agreed upon. This gap increases the risk that the conceptual model may emphasize the wrong entities or relationships, leading to rework when stakeholders review the final model and realize that critical business objectives were misinterpreted or under-represented.

| Criteria            | Description                          |

| Self-validation     | The Fabric Model Conceptual agent must implement a self-validation step that cross-references the generated conceptual model against the original requirements document to confirm that every entity, attribute, and relationship mentioned in the requirements is present in the model. This validation should produce a coverage report showing which requirements are satisfied by which model elements, and flag any requirements that are not addressed. The Fabric Model Data Constraints agent must similarly validate that every business rule, constraint, and compliance requirement from the requirements document is captured in the constraints catalog. If coverage is below 100%, the agent must escalate the gap to the data architect before proceeding. The Business Goals & Use Case Validation Agent (new) must validate that stakeholder feedback has been incorporated into the final goals and KPIs document by comparing the draft version with the approved version and flagging any unresolved comments or conflicts. |
| Boundary definition | The Fabric Model Conceptual agent must NOT define logical or physical data model elements (such as table structures, column data types, indexing strategies, or database-specific constraints) — its scope is strictly limited to conceptual entities, attributes, and relationships. It must NOT invent entities or attributes that are not stated or reasonably inferred from the requirements document. The Fabric Model Data Constraints agent must NOT define technical implementation details (such as database triggers, stored procedures, or ETL logic) — its scope is limited to business rules and logical constraints. The Business Goals & Use Case Validation Agent (new) must NOT proceed with modeling if stakeholders have not approved the goals and KPIs — it must escalate unresolved conflicts and wait for explicit approval. The Domain & Subject Area Architect Agent (new) must NOT create new enterprise-wide subject areas or domains without approval from the enterprise architecture team — it must work within the existing enterprise architecture framework. |
| Known issues log    | The Fabric Model Conceptual agent must log and avoid the following known failure patterns: (1) Omitting implicit relationships that are stated in narrative form rather than explicit entity-relationship statements (such as "applications are linked to campaigns" buried in a paragraph about campaign tracking); (2) Misinterpreting many-to-many relationships as one-to-many when the requirements document uses ambiguous language (such as "customers can have multiple accounts" without clarifying whether accounts can have multiple customers); (3) Failing to identify derived attributes (such as "approval rate" or "conversion rate") that are calculated from other attributes rather than stored directly. The Fabric Model Data Constraints agent must log and avoid: (1) Defining overly restrictive constraints that would block valid business scenarios (such as requiring a credit score for all applicants when some applicants may not have a credit history); (2) Missing compliance-related constraints that are implied but not explicitly stated (such as PII masking requirements for customer names and addresses). The Business Goals & Use Case Validation Agent (new) must log and avoid: (1) Accepting vague or unmeasurable KPIs (such as "improve customer satisfaction" without a specific metric); (2) Failing to identify conflicting priorities among stakeholders (such as one stakeholder prioritizing fraud detection while another prioritizes application approval speed). |
